# Feasibility Report Guide

## Default structure

1. Project background
2. Necessity / why now
3. Current-state analysis
4. Option comparison
5. Business feasibility
6. Technical feasibility
7. Economic feasibility
8. Risk analysis
9. Implementation recommendation
10. Conclusion

## What makes a good feasibility report

- compares options instead of assuming one answer
- separates facts from assumptions
- shows why the recommendation wins
- includes risks that are real, not decorative
- gives a recommendation the reader can act on

## Common weak patterns

- only one option is described, but called a comparison
- benefits are abstract and not tied to business outcomes
- costs are omitted or hand-waved
- recommendation is obvious from the first paragraph and analysis is fake
- risk section is generic filler

## Strong writing moves

- use a comparison table for options
- distinguish short-term and long-term benefits
- mention implementation prerequisites
- state non-ideal trade-offs openly

## Option comparison dimensions

- investment cost
- implementation time
- delivery complexity
- dependency risk
- maintainability
- business impact
- scalability
- compliance/security impact
